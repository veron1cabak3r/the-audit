The Audit

Hypotheses on how fashion brands could do better, built on audited records — but also, how this could actually be them trying their hardest.

A new kind of public record

For most of business history, a company's public record was what the company chose to make public. Quarterly statements. Sustainability reports. Press releases. The version of itself that survived a comms review.

The receipts existed — prices, wages, layoffs, lobbying spend, emissions — but they were scattered across filings, archives, news cycles, and inboxes. Reading them in conversation with each other was the kind of work nobody got paid to do. So nobody did.

That changes now. Not because anything new has appeared, but because reading public information at scale, across companies, across years, has stopped being prohibitive. The work is becoming cheap. And consumers, investors, and employees who care about how businesses actually behave — not just how they describe themselves — are about to have access to a kind of audit that didn't used to exist outside of journalism, regulation, or the boardroom.

This is The Audit.

What it is, exactly

The Audit is a trade-off analysis. Not a score, not a rating, not a ranking. For each of fifteen fashion brands, across whatever conversations are worth having about that brand, we publish where they currently stand, where they could move, and what's already pulling against the move.

The decision to refuse a single number is deliberate. ESG scores tried to summarize corporate behavior with an aggregate, and the predictable thing happened: companies optimized for the score, the score got disconnected from the underlying behavior, and "ESG" became a word people argued about rather than a tool they trusted. We're not going to repeat that.

Every entry has the same shape:

The claim.
What the company said publicly — in earnings calls, ESG reports, press releases, marketing, regulatory filings.
The action.
What the data shows actually happened — from SEC filings, government datasets, public pricing, court records, and other primary sources.
The gap.
Quantified, time-stamped, sourced. Always with context — input costs, peer behavior, the company's own history, the macro environment.
Peer comparison.
How comparable brands handled the same situation. Not commentary — more receipts. H&M vs. Inditex on cotton-price pass-through, or Nike vs. Adidas on living-wage commitments, is a real thing that already happened; we just put it next to each other.
Counterfactual.
The math of an alternative past choice, with assumptions shown. If input costs dropped X% and the brand had passed that through, retail prices would have been Y% lower. Arithmetic, not opinion.
Hypothesis — what they could do better.
Where this brand could move next, grounded in their own numbers — a wage shift, a price cut, a Scope 3 trajectory, a capital reallocation. Specific, quantified, sourced. Forward-looking. We say where the lever is and where it could go.
Counterweight — or how this might already be them trying their hardest.
When a real one exists in the filings, we show it. A regional warehouse already in capex. A multi-year supplier contract. A debt covenant. Or evidence the brand is already at the ceiling of what's structurally possible — the move isn't actually available. When no counterweight exists, we say so plainly. We don't manufacture one.

The reader draws the conclusion. We provide the receipts — what they did, what others did, what an alternative would have looked like, where they could go next, and what's already in motion that complicates the move.

How we decide what's worth noting

We don't decide that a company is "good" or "bad." We notice when what a company says and what a company does drift apart. The vocabulary we use:

Alignment. Claim and action match.

Drift. Small or context-explainable gaps.

Divergence. Significant, sustained gaps between claim and action.

Reversal. Company corrected course — e.g., dropped prices when costs fell.

Pattern. Repeated divergence across categories or time.

Companies that walk their talk look exactly as good as they are. Companies that don't, don't. Nobody has to call anyone a villain.

Beyond noting the gap, we propose: where the brand could move next. The hypothesis is grounded in their own numbers and in what peers have already demonstrated — specific, quantified, sourced. Then we look for what's pulling the other way: concrete commitments, programs, or constraints documented in the filings. When we find one, we show it. When we don't, the hypothesis stands alone.

The eight values

These are the rules The Audit imposes on itself. They're public so that we can be held to them.

  1. Receipts over rhetoric. Every claim sources to a primary document. No exceptions.
  2. Hypothesize, then steelman. For every direction a brand could move, we look for what they're already doing that pulls the other way — or evidence they may already be at the ceiling of what's structurally possible. The reader sees the full trade-off, not a one-sided critique. When there's no real counterweight to a hypothesis, we say so — we don't invent one.
  3. Context is part of the fact. No number stands alone. Inputs, peers, and time are always shown.
  4. Time horizons matter. Patterns over quarters and years tell more than any single event.
  5. Voice from inside, not from PR. Verified employees of audited companies can post public, anonymized rebuttals. The corporation itself is not invited to respond. The humans inside it are.
  6. Visible methodology. How we calculate divergences is documented, reproducible, and open to challenge.
  7. Visible corrections. When we're wrong, the correction sits next to the original claim. Never buried.
  8. No anonymous accusations. If a tip can't be corroborated by primary or independently-reported evidence, it doesn't run.

What gets audited

Audits aren't filed by category. Each audit is its own conversation — about a specific claim, a specific period, a specific decision, a specific event. Some are pricing stories, some are labor stories, some are material-claim stories, some are governance stories, some don't fit any tidy bucket. The audit list for each brand grows as conversations worth having surface.

To give a sense of the kinds of conversations the audit tends to take on:

Pricing decisions

Tariff pass-through, input-cost adjustments, regional pricing fairness, the gap between cost shocks claimed and cost shocks documented.

Labor & supply chain

Garment-worker wages vs. living-wage benchmarks, factory disclosure, supplier-safety commitments, the share of supply chain that's auditable.

Material claims

Sustainability claims (organic, recycled, biodegradable) vs. third-party-verified reality. Traceability of "Conscious," "Coachtopia," "Made With Nature" lines.

Brand vs. behavior

Public commitments (DEI pledges, climate pledges, sponsorship stances) vs. measurable follow-through. Marketing positioning vs. ad-spend reality.

Capital & governance

Layoffs, store closures, write-downs, exec comp, buybacks, M&A transparency. How financial choices were distributed.

These are descriptions, not categories. No audit is forced to fit one. Some span several. Some don't fit any. The audit publishes the conversation that's worth having about the brand at this moment, not the cell that aligns with a predetermined matrix.

Who gets audited first

The first cohort: fifteen of the most-shopped publicly-traded fashion brands. They span the industry's main archetypes — fast fashion, athletic, heritage American, off-price, accessible luxury, mid-tier mall.

Inditex (Zara). H&M. Nike. Lululemon. Adidas. VF Corporation (North Face, Vans, Timberland). Levi Strauss. Ralph Lauren. Gap Inc. (Gap, Old Navy, Banana Republic, Athleta). TJX (TJ Maxx, Marshalls). Tapestry (Coach). Capri Holdings (Michael Kors). PVH Corp. (Calvin Klein, Tommy Hilfiger). American Eagle. Urban Outfitters Inc. (Urban Outfitters, Anthropologie, Free People).

Why these? They're all publicly traded, so disclosure is a legal obligation rather than a choice. Together they touch most American closets. If The Audit can't say something useful about these brands, it can't say something useful about the industry.

Each brand has its own page. Each page lists the conversations worth having about that brand — variable in count, topic-driven, surfaced as they emerge. The Lululemon page is not the Inditex page. Some brands will have three audits, some thirty.

How rebuttals work

The Audit operates on a principle that's slightly unusual: anyone who works at a company being audited can post a public rebuttal. The corporation itself cannot. Their employees, individually, can.

Verification is tied to a working business email and confirmed via an independent professional record. By default, rebuttals appear under "Verified employee at [Company]" — names are held private unless the employee opts in. This is intentional. People who work inside companies should be able to push back on public claims about their employer without putting their livelihood at risk.

If five employees of the same company rebut the same audit entry — and they say different things — readers will see all five. Internal disagreement is information.

Corporations are not invited to respond. We do not solicit comment, host press statements, or maintain a public-relations channel. Companies that wish to respond can do so on their own platforms — press releases, social media, public statements — and they are welcome to. We will not host their reply. The audit publishes receipts; the corporation can speak from its own platform if it chooses.

Removed employee rebuttals are logged publicly with the reason for removal. No quiet deletions.

A note on politics

The Audit is deliberately apolitical in voice, not in subject selection.

Voice means the words we use. We don't write "greedflation" or "woke capitalism" or "ESG scam." We don't imply villainy. We don't write sentences designed to feel satisfying to one tribe and infuriating to another. We describe what we found, and we cite where we found it.

Subject selection follows the data. We audit whoever the receipts support. A company widely admired by progressives gets the same scrutiny as one widely admired by conservatives. Symmetry is not "both sides equally" — it's the same methodology, applied without favor.

This is harder than it sounds. We expect to fail at it sometimes. When we do, we want it noticed.
